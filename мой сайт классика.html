What is classic?

Classical music is an ambiguous concept that is used, depending on the context, in three meanings:

1) Qualitative Assessment of Time-Tested Music: In a broader sense, classical music refers to the music of the past that has stood the test of time and continues to resonate with modern society. This definition includes not only the masterpieces of "high" musical art but also exemplary works from entertainment genres of the past, such as the French and Viennese operettas of the 19th and early 20th centuries and waltzes by Johann Strauss. The term "classics" extends beyond traditional boundaries, encompassing genres like "jazz classics" and "classic pop."

2) Historical and Theoretical Definition of Classicism: In a narrower historical and musical-theoretical context, classical music specifically refers to the period from the second half of the 18th century to the early 19th century, known as the era of classicism. This period, following the baroque era and preceding romanticism, is characterized by clarity, transparency, and a shift towards homophony. The Viennese Classical School, with prominent figures like Haydn, Mozart, and Beethoven, played a central role in shaping classical music. Key features include the development of the eight-bar period, classical harmony, the sonata form, and the establishment of genres like the symphony and quartet.

3) Typological Definition as Academic Music: In a typological sense, classical music is often synonymous with academic music, imitating genres, compositional principles, instrumentation, and performance traditions prevalent in European music from the 17th to the early 20th century. While the 20th century saw conflicts between individual academic composers and traditional norms, this tension reflects a dynamic dialogue within the academic tradition. In contrast, other modern music genres, ranging from pop to jazz, lack a similar dialogue with classical traditions.
